Saydnaya: Inside a Syrian Torture Prison

A precedent study of Forensic Architecture and Amnesty International's 2016 spatial and acoustic reconstruction of Saydnaya military prison, Syria.

Datasheet
Title
Saydnaya: Inside a Syrian Torture Prison (published as "Saydnaya" on Forensic Architecture's site)
Author(s)
Forensic Architecture (Goldsmiths, University of London), in collaboration with Amnesty International. Acoustic investigation by Lawrence Abu Hamdan; principal investigator Eyal Weizman.
Year
2016 — interviews conducted April 2016; presented at the 2016 Venice Biennale
Format
Interactive web-based investigation: a navigable 3D architectural reconstruction paired with acoustic modeling, witness-testimony video, and a downloadable written report.
Intended audience
General public and international press; human-rights monitors and bodies such as the International Syria Support Group; campaigners; and the broader spatial-forensics research community that Forensic Architecture addresses through exhibitions and publications.
Short Description

Saydnaya prison, north of Damascus, has been closed to independent monitors since the start of the Syrian uprising in 2011, and no recent photographs of its interior exist. To document conditions inside, Forensic Architecture and Amnesty International worked with five former detainees, now refugees in Turkey, to rebuild the prison from memory. An architect guided each witness through a 3D modeling process, cross-checking their descriptions of cells, stairwells, and corridors against an earlier model built from satellite imagery and prior interviews.

Because many prisoners were kept blindfolded or in darkness, sound became a primary form of evidence. Lawrence Abu Hamdan led a parallel acoustic investigation, using witnesses' memories of echo, reverberation, and ambient noise to help verify the scale and layout of specific spaces and to reconstruct particular incidents. The resulting website lets a visitor move through the prison by location or by individual witness, folding testimony, video, and model together into a single interface, and links out to Amnesty's campaign for independent access to the prison.
